Reflective Piece

Learning Outcome 4 – Professional Practice and Teamwork

Module: Software Engineering Project Management (SEPM_PCOM7E)

This reflective piece addresses Learning Outcome 4, which focuses on the development and application of professional skills required to operate effectively as a member of a software development team in a virtual and professional environment. The reflection draws upon my experiences throughout the SEPM_PCOM7E module, including teamwork, role selection, communication, and professional self-management.

Context and team environment

The SEPM module was delivered in a distributed, virtual team setting, reflecting contemporary software engineering practice. Collaboration occurred through structured discussions, shared artefacts, and coordinated project activities across multiple units. The environment required clear communication, shared accountability, and effective role allocation to ensure progress under time and quality constraints.

From the outset, the team faced typical challenges associated with virtual collaboration, including asynchronous communication, varying availability, and differences in experience and expectations. These factors made role clarity and capacity management particularly important.

Role selection and professional judgement

In contrast to previous modules and professional contexts where I often assume leadership or coordination responsibilities, I made a deliberate decision in this module not to take on a formal or informal leadership role. This decision was driven by limited personal capacity during the module and an assessment that overcommitment would negatively affect both team outcomes and my own performance.

While this choice reduced immediate cognitive and organisational load, it also limited my level of influence on planning, prioritisation, and decision-making within the team. In retrospect, this contributed to a less prominent contribution than is typical for me and is likely reflected in the final outcome for the module (6.4), compared to my usual academic average (7.4).

Reflection on impact and learning

This experience highlighted that opting out of leadership does not remove responsibility for managing expectations, visibility, and contribution. Although the decision was rational from a capacity perspective, I underestimated the importance of explicitly negotiating an alternative, well-defined role with clearly owned deliverables.

The key learning is that professional effectiveness in team environments is not solely dependent on taking leadership roles, but on ensuring that one’s role—whatever it may be—is visible, bounded, and aligned with both personal capacity and team needs. Passive participation, even when well-intentioned, can reduce perceived contribution and impact outcomes.

Professional development and future application

As a result of this reflection, I have identified concrete actions for future team-based projects. These include early and explicit role negotiation, transparent communication of constraints, and the ownership of smaller but clearly defined responsibilities when full leadership roles are not appropriate.

This approach aligns with professional project management practice and supports sustainable contribution without compromising quality or personal well-being. The reflection directly informs my Professional Development Plan and contributes to my ongoing development as a reflective and responsible software engineering professional.
